Satiety and hunger control

Protein is the most satiating macronutrient. Higher protein meals can reduce hunger, cravings, and overeating, and keto or low-carb choices may blunt ghrelin. This often makes fasting windows easier to follow.

Muscle preservation and body composition

Higher protein intake during fasting supports lean mass retention. Spreading protein across meals can improve fat loss while preserving fat-free mass.

Metabolic and fat-loss effects

Protein has a higher thermic effect, meaning it burns more calories during digestion. Keto or low-carb meals keep insulin lower, supporting fat oxidation and metabolic flexibility, which can make transitions back into fasting smoother.

Practical eating window guidance

A commonly suggested protein range is about 1.6-2.2 g/kg/day for active users (not enforced here). Emphasize whole-food proteins (eggs, meat, fish, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, tofu, tempeh), non-starchy vegetables, and healthy fats. A keto-leaning approach is optional, not required.

What is Intermittent Fasting?

Intermittent fasting is an eating pattern that cycles between periods of eating and fasting. It focuses on WHEN you eat rather than WHAT you eat, allowing your body to enter different metabolic states that promote fat burning, autophagy, and cellular repair.

Benefits of Fasting

‚Ä¢ Fat burning and weight loss
‚Ä¢ Improved insulin sensitivity
‚Ä¢ Enhanced mental clarity
‚Ä¢ Cellular autophagy (cleanup)
‚Ä¢ Reduced inflammation
‚Ä¢ Increased energy levels

Fasting Protocols

Metabolic Phases

0-4h: Fed State - Digesting food
4-8h: Early Fasting - Transitioning to fat burning
8-12h: Fat Burning - Primary fuel source switches
12-16h: Ketosis Begins - Ketone production starts
16-24h: Deep Ketosis - Peak fat burning
24-48h: Autophagy - Cellular cleanup
48-72h: Deep Autophagy - Enhanced cellular repair
72+h: Maximum Autophagy - Advanced cellular renewal

Maintenance Mode

Once you've reached your goals, activate Maintenance Mode for a flexible approach. This mode recommends gentler protocols (8:16, 10:14, 12:12, 14:10) and reduces notification frequency while still tracking your progress. Perfect for sustainable, long-term health.

Near-Failure Training, Calisthenics, and Intermittent Fasting

What is Near-Failure Training?

Near-failure training refers to performing resistance exercises close to technical failure, commonly described as stopping with roughly 0‚Äì3 reps remaining. Research commonly links this approach to increased muscle activation and mechanical tension. Hypertrophy discussions often reference a moderate rep range of approximately 6‚Äì20 reps. Full failure is not required; stopping slightly short is often discussed as sufficient.

Gym Weights and Calisthenics Use the Same Principle

The same near-failure strategy applies to both gym-based and bodyweight training. The underlying driver is progressive overload combined with challenging sets taken close to technical failure.

In the Gym:

Load is increased gradually using free weights or machines. Sets are commonly adjusted to remain challenging within a moderate rep range. Progression occurs when a given weight becomes easier over time.

With Calisthenics:

Progressive overload is achieved by modifying leverage, range of motion, tempo, or adding external load. Bodyweight exercises can be scaled to reach near failure in similar rep ranges as weighted exercises. The effort level, not the equipment, is the primary variable.

Combining Gym and Calisthenics

Many programs mix weighted and bodyweight exercises. Compound lifts and advanced calisthenics movements can both provide a strong muscular stimulus. These approaches are interchangeable across training days. This mixed approach is often discussed alongside intermittent fasting when the goal is fat loss with muscle retention.

Intermittent Fasting Context

Research suggests that intermittent fasting can support fat loss while preserving lean mass. Resistance training provides a muscle-retention signal during periods of calorie restriction. Combining near-failure training with IF may help influence body composition outcomes. Individual responses vary. These are associations observed in research, not guaranteed outcomes.
